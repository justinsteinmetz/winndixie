📖 The Story
Read this first.

What happens?

Opal is 10 years old. She has just moved to a small town called Naomi in Florida with her father, who is a preacher. She doesn't know anyone and feels very lonely.

One afternoon, Opal goes to the supermarket — also called Winn-Dixie. Inside, she finds a large, scruffy dog causing chaos. He has no owner and nowhere to go.

Opal decides to keep him. She names him Winn-Dixie after the supermarket where she found him.

Over the summer, Winn-Dixie helps Opal meet the interesting and lonely people of Naomi. Through him, she makes friends and starts to feel like she belongs somewhere. 🐕

✦ Writer's Craft
Kate DiCamillo is a careful, distinctive writer. These activities look at how she writes, not just what she writes. Similes (A2) → Naming & Repetition (B1+).

Similes — comparing with like or as

A simile compares two different things using the words like or as. DiCamillo uses vivid similes to bring her characters to life. For each one below: what two things is she comparing? What picture does it create?

"He looked like a big piece of old brown carpet that had been left out in the rain."
Who is being described? What two things are being compared?
Winn-Dixie is being compared to old, wet carpet. DiCamillo is showing that he is big, brown, messy, and unloved — like something thrown away. It immediately makes us feel sorry for him.
"The preacher is like a turtle hiding inside its shell, in there thinking about things and not ever sticking his head out into the world."
What does this simile tell us about the Preacher's personality?
The preacher is compared to a turtle hiding in its shell. This shows that he is closed off and withdrawn — he keeps his feelings inside and doesn't open up easily to the world, or to Opal.
"He smelled like a terrible, musty smell, like old things and sadness."
This simile mixes a physical smell with an emotion. What is unusual about comparing a smell to "sadness"? What does it tell us about Winn-Dixie?
Usually we describe smells with physical words — musty, stale, damp. DiCamillo adds "sadness" — an emotion — to a physical sensation. This tells us that Winn-Dixie doesn't just smell unwashed; he carries sadness with him. It also connects him immediately to Opal, who is also sad. The simile does two things at once.

Write a simile describing Winn-Dixie, Opal, or a place in the story. Use like or as.

Names — why do they matter?

Kate DiCamillo said: "I love strange names and I love making them up." Names in this book are carefully chosen. Think about what each name might suggest.

Why do you think the dog is named after a supermarket? What does this tell us about the moment Opal found him?
Naming the dog after the place where she found him keeps that moment alive forever. Even an ordinary, everyday place — a supermarket — becomes meaningful. It suggests that for Opal, finding Winn-Dixie was one of the most important things that ever happened to her.
Throughout the book, Opal usually calls her father "the Preacher", not "Dad". Why might DiCamillo have made this choice?
Calling him "the Preacher" instead of "Dad" shows emotional distance. Opal sometimes sees her father more as a public figure — a man with a role — than as her personal parent. As the story develops and he opens up, this distance slowly begins to close.

Repetition — "because of Winn-Dixie"

The phrase "because of Winn-Dixie" appears many times in the book and is also the title. DiCamillo uses it as a structural device — a way of connecting events to the dog.

★ Extension
These questions are harder. Think carefully. Click to see a suggested answer.

1. The Littmus Lozenge tastes sweet and sad at the same time. Each character tastes something slightly different. Why do you think the same sweet tastes different to different people?

Each character carries their own sadness or loss. The lozenge seems to bring out whatever is already inside the person who tastes it. This suggests that sorrow is personal — everyone's experience of it is shaped by their own history and what they have lost.

2. Gloria Dump's tree is covered in bottles representing her past mistakes. Rather than hiding them, she puts them on display. What does this tell us about how she thinks about regret and forgiveness?

Gloria doesn't hide her mistakes — she acknowledges them openly. The bottles suggest that she believes in accepting your past rather than being ashamed of it. Displaying them could also be a way of forgiving herself: the mistakes are there, they happened, but they don't define who she is now.

3. Opal says: "Just about everything that happened to me that summer happened because of Winn-Dixie." Do you think this is completely true? Is she underestimating her own role in what happened?

Winn-Dixie opened doors — he created the first connections. But it was Opal's own curiosity, kindness, and courage that turned those connections into real friendships. She chose to talk to Gloria Dump, she organised the party, she forgave people. The dog gave her opportunities; she took them.

4. Gloria Dump says: "There ain't no way you can hold on to something that wants to go. You can only love what you got while you got it." What does this mean? How does it connect to Opal's feelings about her mother?

Gloria is saying that trying to hold on to something or someone who has left only causes more pain. It connects directly to Opal's grief about her mother — the message is that rather than spending all her energy missing what's gone, Opal should cherish what she has now: her father, her new friends, Winn-Dixie.

5. Would you want to eat a Littmus Lozenge? It is described as tasting sweet but also sad at the same time. Why might someone want that experience — or not want it?

There is no single answer here — but ask yourself: what does your answer reveal about how you feel about sadness? Do you think it is something to avoid, or something worth understanding? How does your answer compare to what Opal, Miss Franny, or Gloria Dump might say?
